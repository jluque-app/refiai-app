I will start by defining “return” through a simple two-period model : purchase at time 0 and sale at time 1.
***CLICK***
Return, is the change in price of an investment over time, which can either be :
***CLICK***
Positive, meaning that the investment has been profitable.
***CLICK***
Or negative, meaning that the investment has resulted in a loss of money. 
***CLICK***
It is usually calculated as a percentage: Profit (the total money taken out of the investment minus the original total invested) divided by the total amount of money initially put into the investment.
***CLICK***
Now, two important observations:
***CLICK***
Firstly, “Price” and “Return” are opposite sides of the same coin. As the price of an investment increases, the return decreases, and vice versa. An investor looking for a larger return from their investment has to pay a lower price in order to meet their required return.
***CLICK***
Secondly, money is made not when you sell but when you buy an asset. This is because once your purchase price is set, so is your expected return. 
***CLICK***
However your expected return is not completely in your control, as future cash flows are to a large extent dictated by the fluctuations of the market and so it is important to be prepared for unforeseen volatility in the market and your returns. 
***CLICK***

To understand better the previous equation, I will break it down:
There are two components which combine to equal the total return of an investment:
***CLICK***
Income Return – which is the return made from the net operating income, and the
***CLICK***
Appreciation Return – which is the return made from the increasing market value of the property.
***CLICK***
The Income return is often referred to as the yield of an investment and can be expressed as such:
***CLICK***
yt = CFt / Vt-1  (Where y = yield, CF is the operational cash flow and V is the property’s market value)
Is important to investors who concentrate more on income than growth.
***CLICK***
For example: €7,000 / €200,000 = 3.5%
***CLICK***
Ant the Appreciation Return is the cash flow derived from the rise in value of a property, and can be expressed as such:
***CLICK***
Gt = (Vt – Vt-1) / Vt-1  (where G = growth and V is the property’s market value)
Is important to investors who concentrate more on capital growth than income 
***CLICK***
For example: (€205,000 - €200,000) / € 200,000 = 2.5%
***CLICK***
So, as Total Return = Income Return + Appreciation Return
***CLICK***
Therefore, the total return is 3.5% + 2.5% = 6%

Now that you know how to calculate the return of an investment over one period, I will show you how to measure the return of a multi-period investment.
***CLICK***
In the real estate sector, total return is typically measured over multiple time periods because real estate investment is normally long-term. 
***CLICK***
Investors therefore need to calculate the expected long-term horizon of the investment, and the following definition is widely employed:
***CLICK***
Internal Rate of Return or IRR is the single rate that discounts all net cash flows obtained from the investment to a present value that equals the purchase price of the investment.
***CLICK***
There are two approaches to real estate valuation:
***CLICK***
The Income Capitalization Approach (Cap Rate/Yield) and the Discounted Cash Flow (“DCF”) Approach.
***CLICK***
The Income Capitalization Approach is used to…
***CLICK***
calculate how much income an investment will generate over an infinite/perpetual stream of cash flows (cannot be used in finite horizon investment valuation), where…
***CLICK***
Property value = NOI1 / Capitalization Rate (where NOI1 stands for the NOI a year after the initial investment) 
***CLICK***
The Cap Rate captures the opportunity cost of capital of an alternative but similar investment for the investor – it is determined by comparing the cap rates of other similar properties in capital markets.
***CLICK***
Meanwhile the Discounted Cash Flow (“DCF”) Approach is used to…
***CLICK***
Calculate the total return, including income return and appreciation return, of the investment.
***CLICK***
Property value = PV (present value) of all future cash flows, discounted at the required total rate of return (“discount rate”)
***CLICK***
It is only appropriate for finite horizon investment valuation (e.g. 7 years).
***CLICK***
And finally, how do we split the “cap rate” between the investor’s time discount rate and the property’s growth rate?
***CLICK***
To the extent that a real estate investment approximates a constant growth annuity where…
***CLICK***
Y0 = discount Rate, gI = growth Rate, and R0 = Cap Rate
